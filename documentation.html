Project Documentation

Design Process

I started by looking through the movie dataset and thinking about what actually makes a movie “top.” At first I was just going to make a simple chart of the highest rated movies, but that felt too basic and didn’t really say much. Once I noticed there were multiple metrics like rating, popularity, and vote count, I realized those could give completely different results. I considered making separate charts, but that felt repetitive, so I went with one interactive chart where you can switch between them. I sketched a few layouts, but the bar chart with buttons ended up being the clearest and easiest to use.

Rationale of Design Choices

I chose a bar chart because it’s the most straightforward way to compare values across movies, especially since I’m only showing the top 10 at a time. The buttons are the main interactive feature and let the user switch between rating, popularity, and vote count, which makes the visualization more useful than just a static chart. I kept the color consistent so it wouldn’t get confusing when switching views, and I added labels and axes so everything is easy to read without overthinking it. Overall, I kept the design simple so the focus stays on the data.

Findings

After using the visualization, it became clear that “top movies” really depends on how you define it. The highest rated movies are mostly older, critically acclaimed films, while the most popular ones tend to be newer or trending. When looking at vote count, the top movies are usually big, widely watched films that a lot of people have rated. These lists don’t overlap as much as you might expect, which shows that popularity, ratings, and vote count all represent different types of success.
